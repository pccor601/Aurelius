Overcoming Anxiety

Is it acceptable for a Christian to live with anxiety? We know that Christians can grieve; Jesus wept at the grave of Lazarus (John 11:35). Christians can be depressed; Elijah was depressed over the state of Israel (1 Kings 19:10). Christians can be lonely; Adam was lonely until God gave him Eve (Genesis 2:18)...and so on. In John 16:33 Jesus says to his disciples:

"I have said these things to you, that in me you may have peace. In the world you will have tribulation. But take heart; I have overcome the world."

So it is never all doom and gloom for the Christian; in fact, if we read enough of scripture we will soon see that for the Christian the positives far outweigh the negatives. For example, the apostle Paul says in Romans 8:37-39:

"No, in all these things we are more than conquerors through him who loved us. 38 For I am sure that neither death nor life, nor angels nor rulers, nor things present nor things to come, nor powers, nor height nor depth, nor anything else in all creation, will be able to separate us from the love of God in Christ Jesus our Lord."

In this post I want to focus on the problem of anxiety as it has been a problem in my own life for the past couple of months. And there are three passages that speak directly to this subject that warrant our attention: Philippians 4:6-8; Matthew 10:26-31 and 1 Peter 5:6-7.

The steps to peace

Philippians 4:6-8

4 "Rejoice in the Lord always; again I will say, rejoice. 5 Let your reasonableness be known to everyone. The Lord is at hand; 6 do not be anxious about anything, but in everything by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known to God. 7 And the peace of God, which surpasses all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus. 8 Finally, brothers, whatever is true, whatever is honourable, whatever is just, whatever is pure, whatever is lovely, whatever is commendable, if there is any excellence, if there is anything worthy of praise, think about these things."

In verse 4 Paul exhorts his readers to rejoice in the Lord "always". How close do we come to doing this and if we are not close, what prevents us from doing it? Paul answers this question in verse 6 which is all about anxiety. In other words, the greatest joy killer in the Christian life is anxiety. So Paul attacks anxiety head on in this passage by telling us how we can eliminate it from our lives. There are a few steps we can take to achieve this:

1. Pray. This is the first step we need to take if we would remove this powerful enemy of joy. We need to take the matter up with God and the only way to do that is through prayer. To be able to speak to God anywhere and at any time means that we are never alone with our problems.

I used to think that having a mobile phone would solve the problem of loneliness by just ringing up my friends when I needed them. Little did I realise that mobile phones would make it even more difficult to contact people than fixed phones because of the advent of message service. In my frustration with unanswered calls I deduced that the likelihood of a mobile phone being answered is inversely proportional to the importance of the call. No such rule applies to prayer.

2. Supplicate. Our prayers need to be prayers of supplication, i.e. setting our needs before the Lord with fervency and humility. Although God already knows what our needs are he wants us to articulate them before him in prayer. Remember, Christianity is a relationship with God through Christ and communication is the lifeblood of that relationship.

3. Give thanks. To stop our prayers from gravitating into shopping lists we need to thank God for the many blessings he has bestowed upon us in the past. This not only improves the quality of our prayer but also reminds us of just how good God is. In fact the apostle Paul tells us that God is so good to us that Christ died for us while we were unrepentant sinners (Romans 5:8).

So what can we expect from these steps? Paul makes it clear: we can expect "the peace of God". There are a few things we need to know about this peace:

1. It is psychological in nature. There is another kind of peace: relational peace, which we have by virtue of our reconciliation with God. Until we come to Christ in conversion we are at enmity with God and our relationship with him is marked by hostility (John 3:19-21). Reconciliation brings peace with God. But the peace Paul is talking about in this passage is the peace that replaces anxiety which is psychological in nature.

2. It surpasses "all understanding". It does that because it is independent of our circumstances. It is beyond understanding from the perspective of the unbeliever who lives only in the natural world and knows nothing of the supernatural. Being "of God" makes it supernatural because God, who is the source, is supernatural. It is the peace that Paul and Silas had when they sang hymns while incarcerated in a Roman prison (Acts 16:25- 26). It is the peace which Jesus gave to his disciples prior to his crucifixion when he said:

John 14:27: "Peace I leave with you; my peace I give to you. Not as the world gives do I give to you. Let not your hearts be troubled, neither let them be afraid."

Notice how this peace is the antidote to a troubled and fearful heart which is the essence of anxiety.

We need to keep in mind that Philippians 4:6-7 is addressed to Christians who take these verses from the very mouth of God. If we are in any doubt as to their veracity, and hence the veracity of God himself, then James 1:6-8 kicks in:

6 "But let him ask in faith, with no doubting, for the one who doubts is like a wave of the sea that is driven and tossed by the wind. 7 For that person must not suppose that he will receive anything from the Lord; 8 he is a double-minded man, unstable in all his ways."

This means that our prayer becomes dishonouring to God and one that he will absolutely ignore.

There are times when we would like God to answer our prayers audibly but this betrays a certain lack of trust in his written word. If you came to me for counselling concerning a certain matter that was making you anxious, the first thing I would say to you is that God has something to say to you personally about this. Then I would turn to Philippians 4:6-7 and ask you to read it. This approach puts a different complexion on the whole matter and you would take those verses as personally addressed to you.

So let's assume that you have read these verses and you take them directly from God. After a while you would begin to feel better. The truth that God has the matter in hand would slowly settle into your soul and you would begin to feel better. So is this the end of the matter? Probably not because you can still go home and return to all the "what-ifs" that made you anxious in the first place. After all, anxiety is, in most people, a bad habit and habits die hard. So Paul has an antidote for this too and it is comes to us in verse 8 of Philippians 4.

"Finally, brothers, whatever is true, whatever is honourable, whatever is just, whatever is pure, whatever is lovely, whatever is commendable, if there is any excellence, if there is anything worthy of praise, think about these things."

There is something we need to understand about the mind if we are going to reap the benefits of Philippians 4:6-7. The mind is never passive. If you have ever tried to think of nothing you will know what I mean. We catch ourselves thinking about nothing, trying to visualise a blank, wondering if its going to work, remembering the last time we tried this...etc. In other words, the mind is like a perpetual fountain constantly pouring forth thoughts. Even when we are asleep it doesn't rest which is why we dream. So we have to replace the "what-ifs" with positive thoughts, wholesome thoughts and not simply evacuate our minds which is impossible anyway.

So where do we find the true, the honourable, the just, the pure, the lovely, the commendable, the excellent, and the praise worthy? We find all of these things in Christ. He is our first port of call, and the second is the saints of old who can inspire us with their faith. Make knowing Jesus a priority in your life, and read the biographies of the great reformers and saints of the past who exemplified the Christian life in the way they overcame tribulation in their lives. This is what Hebrews 11 is all about.

Finally, we need to recognise that engaging in anxious thoughts is a sin. The Westminster Shorter Catechism defines sin as "any want of conformity to, or transgression of, the law of God." (Q&A 14). Philippians 4:6 includes a prohibition "Do NOT be anxious about anything" (Emphasis mine). To ignore this and engage in anxious thoughts anyway is clearly a sin and if it's a sin then it is possible to stop doing it contrary to how we feel at the time. One reason why anxiety is a sin is because it is a type of fear and Jesus explicitly tells us in Matthew 10:26-31 that fearing anything other than God is idolatry. Let's turn to that passage now.

Matthew 10:26-31

26 “So have no fear of them, for nothing is covered that will not be revealed, or hidden that will not be known. 27 What I tell you in the dark, say in the light, and what you hear whispered, proclaim on the housetops. 28 And do not fear those who kill the body but cannot kill the soul. Rather fear him who can destroy both soul and body in hell. 29 Are not two sparrows sold for a penny? And not one of them will fall to the ground apart from your Father. 30 But even the hairs of your head are all numbered. 31 Fear not, therefore; you are of more value than many sparrows."

Although Matthew 10:26-31 is primarily about fear, as mentioned before, anxiety is a type of fear often fuelled by "what-ifs". Verse 26 tells us explicitly not to fear our persecutors. To put this verse in context, Jesus is encouraging his disciples to preach the gospel openly and without fear because the time would come when they would be vindicated. But it would be a mistake to restrict the application of these verses to persecution because the reasons Jesus gives for not fearing also apply to any potentially fear provoking situation, except one. We are entitled, and even obligated, to fear God when we sin against him. How so? Because, says Jesus, God can "destroy both body and soul in hell".

It is interesting to note from this verse that only God has the power to cast someone into hell. Our earthly enemies, on the other hand, can only do to us what God permits. Jesus uses the example of the sparrows to illustrate God's sovereign love for his people; his argument goes like this: if God loves sparrows to the point where they cannot fall to the ground without his consent, how much more does he love us when not even a single hair can fall from our heads without his consent.

So what does Matthew 10:26-31 tell us about how we can deal with anxiety in general? The solution is two fold:

1. God's love for us is extensive in that it is incomprehensibly great. In John 3:16 we read that the Father gave no less than his only begotten Son for our salvation. In purchasing us by his blood Jesus now owns us as objects of inestimable value. Indeed, the writers of the Heidelberg Catechism understood this perfectly well when they wrote Q&A 1 of Lord's Day 1:

"Question: What is your only comfort in life and in death? Answer: That I am not my own, but belong—body and soul, in life and in death—to my faithful Savior, Jesus Christ. He has fully paid for all my sins with his precious blood, and has set me free from the tyranny of the devil. He also watches over me in such a way that not a hair can fall from my head without the will of my Father in heaven; in fact, all things must work together for my salvation. Because I belong to him, Christ, by his Holy Spirit, assures me of eternal life and makes me wholeheartedly willing and ready from now on to live for him."

2. God's love for us is intensive in that is incomprehensibly detailed. This means that there is no part of us that is not the subject of his saving love. To number every one of our hairs is to treat every one of them as important. We get them cut by a barber and they fall out naturally in time but this is not without our heavenly father's consent. So if God can care for the individual hairs of our head how much more will he care for whatever matter besets us with anxiety. We can confidently leave any matter in his hands and get on with the task of serving him with the joy that is our birthright.

Submitting to His care

1 Peter 5:6-11

6 "Humble yourselves, therefore, under the mighty hand of God so that at the proper time he may exalt you, 7 casting all your anxieties on him, because he cares for you. 8 Be sober-minded; be watchful. Your adversary the devil prowls around like a roaring lion, seeking someone to devour. 9 Resist him, firm in your faith, knowing that the same kinds of suffering are being experienced by your brotherhood throughout the world. 10 And after you have suffered a little while, the God of all grace, who has called you to his eternal glory in Christ, will himself restore, confirm, strengthen, and establish you. 11 To him be the dominion forever and ever. Amen."

We might be tempted to think that 1 Peter 5:6-11 is about relief from anxiety except that the principal clause is an imperative to be humble. So why the emphasis upon humility? The next clause gives us the answer, "so that at the proper time he"... [God]..."may exalt you". To be exalted we must first be humbled, and it is only as we are humbled that we can cast our anxieties on him.

Anxiety is, in a sense, an arrogant posture to adopt since it seeks to take on what only a sovereign God can handle. It is to encroach upon His domain because the future is exclusively His to control and not ours. Clearly, if we had any control we would not be anxious at all but would do whatever we could to deal with the problem. But the anxiety spoken of here is the fear of something over which we have no control so the logical thing to do here is to leave it to the one who controls all things, and that is the sovereign God of scripture.

In verse 8 of our text Peter goes on to describe the activity of the devil who, like a lion on the prowl, goes about looking for prey. I would suggest that by the time you are filled with anxiety he has already taken a decent size bite out of you. But it doesn't have to end here for Peter also says in verse 9, "Resist him, firm in your faith". Although we might not have any control over the cause of our anxiety, we do have control over the anxiety itself by taking the steps described in chapter 1. We notice that there are three things the Christian needs to know in order to comply with the imperatives of 1 Peter 5:6- 11. Let's consider them separately:

1. God cares for us. This might sound pretty basic as most people, even many non-Christians, believe in a benevolent God. But there is a difference between belief in a benevolent god and trusting in Christ as Lord and Savior. A Christian knows that God cares for him because He has given His only begotten Son to die for him. This was a profound act of love that was unsurpassed in its extent. And if God could love us to such an extent when we were unrepentant sinners, how much more precious have we become to him now that we are justified by His blood and sanctified by his Spirit?

2. Our problems are not unique. They are the experience of countless Christians throughout the world many of whom have learned to cope without the burden of anxiety. Yes, Paul has persecution primarily in mind but God does not restrict his comforts to persecuted Christians; it's just a matter of accessing those comforts in whatever circumstance we find ourselves. 3. Our problems are only ever temporary. This is the meaning of verse 10:

"And after you have suffered a little while, the God of all grace, who has called you to his eternal glory in Christ, will himself restore, confirm, strengthen, and establish you."

These verses apply particularly when we are called to a period of suffering whether that be one of sickness, financial loss, death of a loved one, alienation or persecution. God will restore us in due time, confirming our status as children, strengthening our faith for future tribulation and establishing us in the path of obedience. Does this mean that Christians cannot suffer permanent loss or even death? No, but even if it ends in death, such is not a victory for the devil but a deliverance from suffering and a portal into the very presence of the Lord Jesus Christ Himself.

Finally in verse 11 we are reminded that God has the dominion now and forever. It is important to be reminded of this because, as pointed out earlier, much anxiety is an attempt on our part to wrest that dominion away from God and to ourselves which cannot work, leaving us paralysed with fear.

Getting Busy

It was not inconsequential that the anxiety I engaged in for a protracted period also coincided with a period of relative inactivity. It is interesting that when God created Adam he set him in a garden and put him to work (Genesis 2:15). This tells us that man was made to work and to ignore this imperative is to invite the mischief of idle hands. We could substitute "minds" for "hands" and the effect would be the same. And if we set our minds on the task of mental renewal as per Romans 12:2 we won't have time to be anxious.

In Romans 12:11 we read, "Do not be slothful in zeal, be fervent in spirit, serve the Lord." We can't serve the Lord by sitting around doing nothing. Being an active member of a church is one important way in which we can serve the Lord. The bible knows nothing of nominal membership. And if anxiety is an evil then it is something that needs to be overcome and doing good is another weapon in our arsenal to slay the dragon of anxiety (Romans 12:21). There is plenty in Romans 12 that qualifies as "good, acceptable and perfect" so we will never run out of things to do. Some of these things include the following: acts of mercy (verse 8); being constant in prayer (verse 12); contributing to the needs of the saints (verse 13); and being hospital (verse 13).

In conclusion, if you have a problem with anxiety then you are being cheated out of your birthright. We need to recognise it for what it is: a debilitating parasite that prevents us from serving the Lord with all the joy that such service requires. But this enemy can be vanquished with the resources that God makes available to us in Christ. We will close with a quote from 1 John 4:4.

"Little children, you are from God and have overcome them, for he who is in you is greater than he who is in the world."
