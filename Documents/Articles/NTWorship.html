Towards a New Testament Understanding of Worship

© Charlie Cornford 1990

[A Theological paper prepared by Charlie Cornford and published in the November 1990 edition of the Reformed Journal]

One cannot help wondering whether much of what is written and spoken about worship today takes sufficient cognisance of the redemptive/historical progress of biblical revelation. The reasoning is plain enough: the Israelites met in the temple (God's house) to worship God according to the Levitical sacrificial system; Christians now meet in God's house (the "church") to worship God by offering up sacrifices of prayer, scripture reading, conscionable hearing of the word, singing of psalms ... etc. Indeed we even call the collection "the offering". The logic is attractive but just how sound is the theology?

The real question to be answered is this: what is the New Testament counterpart to the Old Testament sacrificial system? Or alternatively, is the "worship service" really the cultus1 of the New Testament church? To answer these questions we must clearly establish what kind of relationship obtains between Old and New Testament forms. An important key is given to us in Colossians 2:16, 17.

"Do not therefore allow anyone to judge you in (respect of) food and drink or (in respect of) a solemn feast or a new moon or sabbaths which are a shadow (σκια) of the coming things, but the body is of Christ."

The reference to σαββατων indicates that Paul is describing Old Testament cultic forms. Paul then would have us relate Old Testament to New in terms of shadow and body (substance - NASB). This relationship of shadow to substance is expanded upon in the book of Hebrews and especially in Heb. 8:4, 5.

"If therefore he were upon the earth, he would not be a priest at all, there being those who offer the gifts according to the law; who are serving a copy and shadow (σκια) of the heavenlies, just as Moses (was) warned when he (was) about to erect the tabernacle, for "See, he says, that you make all things according to the type (pattern - NASB) which was shown to you on the mountain."

Here the worship (λατρευουσιν) of the Old Testament is described in terms of copies (υποδειγματι) and shadows (σκια). The "heavenlies", on the other hand, denote the heavenly realities (or substance) which are identified with the high priestly work of Christ in the heavens. Of particular importance in Heb. 8:5 is the use of the word λατρευουσιν from latreuo, a word used exclusively throughout the book of Hebrews to denote cultic worship2. We may deduce therefore that it is the heavenly realities, i.e., the high priestly work of Christ, that is the "cultus" of the New Testament church.

So where do we get the notion that the cultus of the New Testament church is the activities of the believers assembled together on Sunday? It is suggested that such a view results from a simplistic linear approach to inter-testamental application that fails utterly to take into account the shadow-substance relationship between the two testaments. That such a view is alive and well even among Reformed writers today is evidenced by such cultic descriptions as "the worship service is the 'holy of holies', the inner sanctuary of the Church" ... [it is] ... "the gathering of the saints around the throne of God3. It is also to be expected that when we insist on calling our congregational meeting places "the church" or "the house of God" that we finish up with some kind of temple worship approach.

So how do we describe the public assemblies of the New Testament church if not in cultic terms? A close look at those New Testament passages which specialize in this area would be most instructive. Throughout 1 Corinthians 14, a passage devoted entirely to the matter of Christian public assemblies, cultic language is conspicuous by its absence. The only time worship is mentioned is in connection with the stranger who falls down on his face under conviction of sin and worships4 God because he recognizes God to be in the midst of the congregation. Were we to see in this act of worship an imperative for us today then we too, to be consistent, would have to bow down in the public assemblies like the Moslems. But the point Paul is simply making here is not that we should be bowing down in our assemblies but that our aim in the assemblies is to bring about changes in people through edifying behavior. Indeed, the word that occurs over and over again in this passage is not proskuneo (to bow down) or latreuo or threskeia (religion) or eusebeia (piety), or even leitourgia (service) but oikodomeo (to edify) and its cognates, seven times in all! In verse 26 Paul sums up the purpose of their meeting together, " Let all things be done for edification" (oikodomen).

This is a strange conclusion for Paul to make if the whole purpose of gathering together is to worship God. After all, don't we tell our parishioners that they do not come to church for what they can get out of it but rather for what they can put into it, i.e., to worship God? If this were correct then we would expect Paul to argue along these lines: "Why are you offering to God the blemished sacrifice of confusion? Don't you realize that you are on holy ground and that you are desecrating the sanctuary by your behavior? Who commanded this strange fire from your hand anyway? Haven't you heard of the Regulative Principle of Worship?".

An equally compelling argument is Paul's objection to uninterpreted tongues. In verse 2 he says of the uninterpreted tongues speaker, "For the one speaking in a tongue does not speak to men but to God" ... (NASB). If the main purpose of the public assemblies is to cultically worship God then 1 Cor. 14:2, far from being an argument against uninterpreted tongues, actually becomes an argument FOR the inclusion of uninterpreted tongues since cultic worship is Godward. We can see therefore how a cultic view of the public assemblies of the church turns this passage on its head.

Heb. 10:24, 25 is another passage much used to support the idea of cultic worship of the New Testament church. Again it needs to be said that the Greek words for "worship", or any other cultic language for that matter, are completely absent from this passage too:

"And let us consider one another toward incitement of love and good deeds, not forsaking the assembling of ourselves, as is the custom of some, but encouraging (one another), and so much more as long as you see the day approaching near."

But as in 1 Cor. 14, what is obvious here is that the purpose of the episunagogen is mutual stimulation and encouragement; in other words, edification and not worship as is generally supposed. To sum up what has been said so far then, the cultus of the New Testament church must be understood as having its focus in the high priestly work of our Lord Jesus Christ. The public assemblies, on the other hand, have as their focus the "equipping of the saints for the work of service, to the edification (oikodomen) of the body of Christ." (Eph. 4:12). In what sense then do the New Testament saints worship? If we understand that the work of Christ is substitutionary then we will have no difficulty in seeing that the latreia of Christ is also the latreia of the church. Christ is our sacrifice, our high priest, and his body is our temple of which we are members if indeed we are one with him. He alone offers "purity of worship" to the Father and his worship is therefore acceptable. But there is another sense in which the New Testament church worships, even cultically, and we turn to Rom. 12:1,2 for a description of that worship.

"I encourage you therefore, brethren, by the compassion of God, to present your bodies (as) a living sacrifice holy (and) well pleasing to God, (which is) your metaphorical worship."

In contrast to those passages which are descriptive of the public assemblies of the church, this passage, which is a description of an obedient lifestyle, is replete with cultic language and imagery. We have the words thusian - sacrifice; hagian - holy, latreian - worship. That the entire lifestyle of the Christian is his latreia is also shown in other passages such as Heb. 12:28 - 13:6.

"Therefore, since we receive a kingdom which cannot be shaken, let us show gratitude by which we may offer (latreuomen) to God an acceptable service with reverence and awe for our God is a consuming fire." (NASB)

Here we notice in verses 28 and 29 that the Greek word translated "we may offer" is latreuomen, from latreuo. So what is this latreia which we are to give to God? Let's read on, keeping in mind that the chapter divisions are arbitrary.

1 "Let brotherly love continue. 2 Do not forget to entertain strangers, for by so doing some have unwittingly entertained angels. 3 Remember the prisoners as if chained with them — those who are mistreated — since you yourselves are in the body also. 4 Marriage is honorable among all, and the bed undefiled; but fornicators and adulterers God will judge. 5 Let your conduct be without covetousness; be content with such things as you have. For He Himself has said, “I will never leave you nor forsake you.” (NKJV)

If the "worship service" were the latreia of the New Testament church then we would expect the writer to the Hebrews, in keeping with the regulative principle, to begin a list of do's for such services. But this is exactly what we do not find. Instead he begins to tell us how we ought to live. This is in perfect harmony with the latreia5 of Rom. 12:1.

What I have endeavoured to show is that the idea of the public assemblies in which prayer, scripture reading, preaching and the like are engaged in is somehow the "worship" of the New Testament saints, is utterly foreign to the New Testament. It is as foreign as the concept of the congregational meeting place being considered as the "house of God". But if the entire life of the New Testament saint is his worship then it follows that this life does not exclude what he does in the public assemblies of the church. Prayer, scripture reading, and singing of the psalms are certainly part of his overall worship but they are by no means the cultus of the ecclesia, and that is the point. So when the saints gather together in their assembly complex (or auditorium - call it what you will, but don't call it the house of God!) let them observe the Pauline directives, "Let all things be done for edification" (1 Cor. 14:26) and let them be done "properly and in an orderly manner" (V.40).

In conclusion, let us be clear about the particular place of our public assemblies within the much broader framework of New Testament worship. We meet for the purpose of edification (1 Cor. 14); mutual encouragement and stimulation to love and good deeds (Heb. 10:24, 25); equipping of the saints for the work of service (diakonias) (Heb. 4:12); and devotion to the apostle's teaching and to fellowship, to the breaking of bread and to prayer (Acts 2:42). But the latreia as practiced by the New Testament church is something far broader encompassing the totality of an obedient life-style.But even this latreia, according to Rom. 12:1 is really only metaphorical (logiken). It is only in a metaphorical and not literal sense that we present our bodies as living and holy sacrifices to God. The literal sacrifice is the self-surrender of our Lord Jesus Christ upon the cross of Calvery and his entrance into the heavenly holy of holies as the perfect lamb. And it is only as we emulate this self-giving act of Christ in an obedient life of service that it can be said that we are performing latreia.

There is much more that can be said on this topic but it is hoped that what has been said will go towards a better theology of New Testament worship than we have seen over the years.
